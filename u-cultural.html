INTRODUCTION OF UKRAINIAN CULTURE

The Eastern Orthodox Church, Ruthenian Greek Catholicism, and Ukrainian Greek Catholicism, as well as practices from Slavic mythology, all had a significant influence on Ukrainian culture. Prior to the Soviet Union, other East Slavic cultures, like Russian and Belarusian culture, had a significant influence on Ukrainian culture.

MANNERS AND CUSTOMS

A warm, firm handshake, maintaining direct eye contact, and saying your name are standard greetings.

On birthdays and throughout the Orthodox Christmas season, Ukrainians give gifts to their loved ones and close friends. Gifts do not have to be pricey. The act of giving the gift itself is significant since it represents friendship.

It is customary to offer something, like as cake, flowers, or a bottle of imported liquor, when you are invited to a Ukrainian's home for dinner.

In the culture, toasting is customary whenever three or more people share a meal. Ukrainians are wary of those who don't consume alcohol. Having said that, it is preferable to cite a medical issue as a justification as opposed to starting to drink and falling behind your Ukrainian colleagues. A common toast is "za vashe zdorovya" , which means "to your health" .

PERCEPTIONS OF TIME

Ukrainians have a fair amount of time awareness. Rural and urban environments differ unmistakably from one another. The capital time is something that people in Kiev are more conscious of. Despite the fact that occasionally 5–10 minutes are probably acceptable.

When speaking to one another, Ukrainians come closer to one another. Loud speech is frowned upon when it occurs in public. Ukrainians frequently converse in a lower voice and stay closer to one another.

RELIGION

Ukraine's primary religion is Christianity. Roman Catholicism and Islam are also practiced, however they are minority religions.

SYMBOLISM

"The trident of Volodymyr the Great"

Ukrainians can trace their nation's origins to the 9th century, when Kyivan Rus' was established with Kyiv as its capital. It was a sizable medieval state that connected numerous Slavic tribes and stretched from the Baltic Sea in the north to the Black Sea in the south. Grand Prince Volodymyr the Great and his son, Grand Prince Yaroslav the Wise, oversaw Kyivan Rus' , which was ruled by the Rurikid dynasty, and they brought about the country's greatest prosperity.

The commercial network of Kyivan Rus' was highly developed, and it was a component of the crucial trade route "from the Varangians to the Greeks" that linked Scandinavia and Byzantium during the Middle Ages. Volodymyr the Great produced his own coinage, like many other kings of that era. 400 or more of them may exist.

EDUCATION

All kids between the ages of 7 and 15 must attend nine years of compulsory education. Upper secondary school or a career path are options after a comprehensive school. Upper secondary school: a three-year general education program that culminates in the matriculation examination, which grants successful students admission to universities and other higher learning institutions. The current vocational education system offers both general secondary and vocational programs, with distinct pathways for those who have graduated from high school and those who have not. It is being reformatted.

PANDEMIC ERA

With the use of online distant learning tools and television broadcasts of video classes, Ukraine additionally put in place mechanisms to facilitate remote teaching and learning. During the COVID-19 issue, organizations like EdCamp Ukraine created peer-to-peer learning and online professional development opportunities for teachers to connect virtually and share experiences with online learning. In order to inform teachers, administrators, students, and parents about the standards for safe and sustained learning under COVID-19 in the 2020–21 school year, Ukraine also organized communication programs, such as "Schools, We Are Ready, " in partnership with UNICEF.

IMPACT OF WAR

Displacement of educators and students. More than 6 million Ukrainians have fled to nearby nations as a result of the war in their country. This includes more than 25,000 teachers (6% of all teachers in the nation) and close to 665,000 students (16% of all students enrolled). Another 8 million Ukrainians are internally displaced.1 The majority of refugees and IDPs are children, women, and older people. More than 74% of IDPs live in households with kids.

Displacement has had a significant impact on education service delivery, along with damage and destruction of educational facilities. As of May 6, 2022, according to the Ministry of Education and Science of Ukraine (MoES), 1,635 schools and universities (5% of total) have been damaged from the war and 126 have been destroyed.

Impact on Learning Opportunities and Learning Results. War or pandemic-related school closures can have long-lasting negative effects on learning because they affect all aspects of a student's learning opportunity: I less time spent learning, (ii) poorer quality instruction delivered remotely or online, and (iii) less content covered during instruction.

APPROACH TO TIME AND PRIORITIES

  • Due to Ukraine's moderately stringent adherence to deadlines and schedules, there may occasionally be some wiggle room.
  • It is advisable to emphasize the significance of the agreed-upon deadlines and how it may affect the rest of the business while working with Ukrainians in order to accomplish effective cross-cultural management.
  • Expanding globally and across cultures may have made some managers more aware of the necessity of enforcing deadlines, increasing the likelihood that they will be reached.

DECISION MAKING

  • The majority of organizations continue to be hierarchical, with staff members expected to submit to anyone in a position of power.
  • The age and prestige, which is indicated by position or fortune, are highly respected in Ukraine.
  • By Western standards, the managerial style could come across as autocratic or dictatorial.
  • Managers anticipate that their employees will blindly adhere to established protocols.
  • Since the educational system for so long emphasized memorization, many employees lack the ability to solve problems.
  • It's common for subordinates to be reluctant to challenge a manager's directives.
  • Many senior managers would rather avoid making a decision than take the chance of making the wrong one.

BOSS OR TEAM PLAYER?

  • The communal characteristics of the previous era, where groups and work units frequently gathered together to share ideas and make plans, left behind a culture of teamwork in post-communist nations. The workforce became disinterested and cynical as a result of the plans' seldom implementation or results.
  • Many members of the elder generation are still feeling the impacts now, which results in a lack of motivation and energy. The younger generation, however, is vibrant and seems eager to take on many of the difficulties and opportunities that come their way.
  • They will work in groups and exchange ideas, but intercultural awareness will be necessary, and it should be recognized that they will need coaching throughout the process.

COMMUNICATION AND NEGOTIATION STYLES

  • Ukrainians prefer to work with people they can trust, therefore they take their time building relationships.
  • A necessary cross-cultural quality may be patience. To prevent any potential cross-cultural misunderstandings, bring in your own translators for meetings and negotiations.
  • However, the most senior Ukrainian is typically the spokesperson in the majority of negotiations.
  • Negotiation can be difficult for Ukrainians. In an effort to pressure you into making concessions, the opposing party will wait more if you let them know that you have a deadline.
  • In an effort to persuade you to modify your perspective, Ukrainians may lose their cool, leave the meeting, or threaten to break off the relationship.
